# Time as Governance: Intersubjective Coordination and Subjective Experience

## The Inherently Intersubjective Nature of Time

Time is inherently intersubjective. It is a coordination mechanism that exists only through collective agreement and shared frameworks. We experience it subjectively, but the framework itself requires social coordination to function.

This makes time a governance framework—an intersubjective coordination structure that people experience and participate in based on their circumstances, abilities, and perspectives.

## Understanding Coordination Through Time

Understanding time this way reveals how coordination systems work: the framework is shared and intersubjective, but individual experiences vary. Effective coordination must account for this variation—recognizing that the same "10 minutes" or "2 hours" means different things to different people based on their context, capabilities, and circumstances.

Just as governance coordinates activity while respecting individual differences, time coordinates our activity while we each experience its passage uniquely.

## The Parallel to Governance

Time operates as governance does in the Civicism framework:

- **Intersubjective Framework**: Both time and governance require collective agreement and shared frameworks to function
- **Subjective Experience**: Both are experienced differently by individuals based on their circumstances, abilities, and perspectives
- **Coordination Mechanism**: Both enable collective activity by providing shared structures for coordination
- **Variation Accommodation**: Both must account for individual differences while maintaining the shared framework

The same person who takes 15 minutes to complete a task while another takes 2 hours experiences time differently, just as people experience governance differently based on their social location, resources, and capabilities. Yet both operate within shared frameworks that enable coordination.

## Implications for System Design

This understanding of time informs how we design coordination systems. We must recognize that:

1. **Shared frameworks are necessary** for coordination to occur
2. **Individual variation is inevitable** and must be accommodated
3. **Effective coordination** balances the shared framework with recognition of individual differences
4. **Transparency** about how the framework operates enables better participation

Time teaches us that coordination mechanisms can be both universal (in their framework) and particular (in their experience). Governance systems should operate the same way—providing shared structures for coordination while recognizing and accommodating the diverse ways people experience and participate in those structures.
