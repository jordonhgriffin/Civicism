# Operationalizing Vortical Progression with empirical data

**The empirical infrastructure to operationalize both axes exists, but no single database spans the full −10,000 BC to 2026 range, and the z-axis "character" dimension—distinguishing constructive from calcified governance—represents the most significant data gap.** Roughly 40 databases can be spliced together to construct the y-axis (Capability), with strong multi-dimensional coverage from 1870 onward and sparse-but-usable archaeological proxies reaching back 12,000 years. The z-axis (Governance) is harder: every major governance dataset treats high scores as normatively positive, requiring decomposition rather than direct use. The critical breakthrough is that V-Dem's 531 indicators can be recombined into separate density and character composites, and a recent operationalization of North-Wallis-Weingast's open-access-order framework provides the closest existing proxy for the constructive-vs-calcified distinction. One empirical warning: Seshat's principal component analysis finds that **~75% of variance in social complexity loads onto a single dimension**, suggesting capability and governance may be more tightly correlated historically than the two-axis model assumes.

## The deep-history layer: archaeological proxies before writing

For the period −10,000 BC to roughly −4,000 BC, quantitative data is extremely thin. Five databases provide the best available proxies, though all require significant interpretation.

**Seshat: Global History Databank** (seshat-db.com) is the single most integrative resource. It codes **~1,500 variables across 861 polities** in 10 macro-regions, with temporal coverage from roughly 4000 BCE to 1900 CE at 100-year intervals. Variables directly mapping to the y-axis include polity population, territory, largest settlement size, infrastructure (roads, bridges, canals, irrigation), information systems (writing, script types, scientific literature), military technology (49 warfare variables from bronze tools to plate armor), and economic indicators (money types, markets, trade infrastructure). For the z-axis, Seshat tracks administrative hierarchy levels, religious hierarchy, formal legal codes, courts, judges, bureaucratic characteristics (examination systems, merit promotion), degree of centralization, and—critically for governance character—limits on executive power, property rights, discrimination, and slavery. The CrisisDB module captures crisis consequences including elite extermination, popular uprising, and regime collapse, which can proxy governance failure. Seshat data is freely available under CC BY-NC-SA, downloadable as spreadsheets or via API. Its primary limitation for this framework is that data is organized by polity, not by sub-populations within polities, making intersectional disaggregation impossible.

**HYDE 3.3** (History Database of the Global Environment, PBL Netherlands) provides the deepest continuous time series: **global population, cropland, grazing land, and urbanization at 5-arc-minute spatial resolution from 10,000 BCE to 2023 CE**. Population density serves as a proxy for carrying capacity and subsistence efficiency; urban population proxies organizational capability and economic surplus. HYDE is freely downloadable from Utrecht University's data repository and provides three scenarios (baseline, lower, upper) reflecting the enormous uncertainty for early periods—global population estimates range from roughly 4.4 million at 10,000 BCE to over 7 billion today. For the z-axis, urbanization rates correlate with state formation, but HYDE cannot capture governance structure directly.

**The Global History of Health Project** (Ohio State University, economics.osu.edu) provides bioarchaeological data spanning **the late Paleolithic (~10,000 years ago) to the early 20th century**, with over 60,000 skeletons from 350+ European localities and 12,000 individuals from the Western Hemisphere. Variables include stature (the single best cumulative health indicator), dental health, nutritional deficiency markers, infection rates, degenerative joint disease (proxy for labor intensity), and trauma patterns (proxy for violence). This maps cleanly to the y-axis people-level dimension and is one of the few deep-history sources with **individual-level sex disaggregation**, enabling gender-based capability analysis.

**The Atlas of Cultural Evolution** (Peregrine, accessible through D-PLACE) covers **289 archaeological traditions across 12,000 years** using an adapted Murdock-Provost Cultural Complexity Scale. Ten variables—writing/records, fixity of residence, agriculture, urbanization, technological specialization, transport, money, population density, political integration, and social stratification—are coded on 3-point scales. Factor analysis separates a "Scale Factor" (population, urbanization, agriculture) mapping to y-axis from a "Complexity Factor" (writing, technology, political integration, stratification) mapping to z-axis.

**D-PLACE** (d-place.org, Max Planck Institute) aggregates the Ethnographic Atlas (1,264+ societies), Standard Cross-Cultural Sample (186 societies, 2,000+ variables), and Binford Hunter-Gatherer Dataset into a freely accessible, open-access platform. Political hierarchy levels, social stratification, and property systems proxy the z-axis; subsistence type, population density, and technology measures proxy the y-axis. The limitation is that these data are primarily synchronic (snapshot at time of ethnographic observation, mostly 19th–20th century), not diachronic.

For the period before 4000 BCE, **governance data is essentially absent** from quantitative databases. Only the Atlas of Cultural Evolution and archaeological inference from settlement hierarchy complexity provide proxies. The Allen Ancient DNA Resource (Harvard Dataverse, 10,000+ ancient individuals with genome-wide data from 45,000 BP onward) can supplement with population health and kinship structure indicators but does not directly code for governance.

## Capability databases from antiquity through present

The y-axis can be populated with increasingly dense data from 1 CE onward, reaching comprehensive multi-dimensional global coverage by 1870.

**The Maddison Project Database 2023** (rug.nl/ggdc, CC BY 4.0) provides GDP per capita in 2011 international PPP dollars for **169 countries from 1 CE to 2022**. The 2023 version expanded pre-industrial coverage to approximately 2,800 data points, up from just 158 in Maddison's original dataset. Annual series exist for England from 1270 (Broadberry et al.), Holland from 1347, Italy from 1300, Japan from 725 CE, and Spain from 1270. Pre-1500 coverage outside these countries consists of sparse benchmark estimates (e.g., Roman Empire at 1 CE, China at 980 CE). Known limitations include the problematic "indirect method" for estimating pre-modern GDP from wages and urbanization—this method finds only **50% growth in English GDP per capita from 1250 to 1820**, while Broadberry's direct output approach finds **~110% growth**. No sub-population disaggregation is possible.

**The OECD "How Was Life?" project** (Volumes I and II, freely available; underlying data at clio-infra.eu) is the most comprehensive multidimensional historical well-being dataset, covering **10 dimensions from 1820 to 2020**: GDP per capita, real wages, educational attainment, life expectancy, height, personal security (homicide rates), political institutions, environmental quality, income inequality, and gender inequality. Volume II added working hours, biodiversity, social spending, extreme poverty, and composite measures. Coverage spans 25 major countries, 8 world regions, and the global aggregate. This maps excellently to both people-level and domain-level capability sub-dimensions.

**Prados de la Escosura's Augmented Human Development Index** (espacioinvestiga.org, also on Our World in Data) provides the closest existing proxy for era-relative capability: a composite of healthy life, knowledge, living standards, and civil/political freedom for **up to 162 countries from 1870 to 2020**. Unlike the standard UNDP HDI (available only from 1990), the AHDI uses logarithmic normalization and a geometric mean, capturing the insight that improvements at high levels are more significant. It draws on Riley's life expectancy estimates, Barro-Lee educational data, Maddison GDP, and V-Dem democracy scores.

**Barro-Lee Educational Attainment Dataset** (barrolee.com) covers **146 countries from 1950 to 2015** (with a long-run extension to 1870 for 89 countries), disaggregated by sex and 5-year age group across seven education levels from no formal schooling through complete tertiary. This is the strongest long-run source for gender-disaggregated human capital.

**Allen's Global Price and Income History Group** (gpih.ucdavis.edu) provides real wages of unskilled laborers expressed as "welfare ratios" (daily wage ÷ cost of subsistence basket) across Europe from 1375, the Ottoman Empire, India from 1600, China from 1738, and Latin America from the 1500s. **Clark's England series** extends to 1209. These are limited to unskilled male wages in specific occupations but provide the longest continuous living-standards data outside GDP estimates.

**Riley (2005)** compiled regional and global life expectancy at birth estimates from **1800 to 2001** using 700+ sources, finding global life expectancy of roughly **28.5 years in 1800 rising to ~65 by 2000**. These estimates, though highly uncertain for pre-1900 non-European regions, underpin both the AHDI and Our World in Data's life expectancy series.

The **Long-Term Productivity Database** (longtermproductivity.com, Bergeaud, Cette & Lecat) provides Total Factor Productivity for **23 advanced economies from ~1890 to present**—the standard measure of technology's contribution to growth. The **Penn World Table** (University of Groningen) extends TFP coverage to most countries from 1950.

## Governance databases and the density-vs-character decomposition

The z-axis presents the framework's hardest measurement challenge. Every major governance dataset conflates governance quantity with governance quality—a fundamental mismatch with Vortical Progression's concept of "The Grip."

**V-Dem** (v-dem.net, v16 released 2025) is the most powerful resource for z-axis construction because its **531 indicators can be decomposed** into separate density and character composites. Covering **202 polities from 1789 to present**, V-Dem provides five high-level democracy indices (electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, egalitarian) plus 93+ mid-level component indices. For governance density/presence, the key variables include state authority over territory, state fiscal capacity, local/regional government existence, legislature structure, and bureaucratic presence indicators. For governance character (the constructive-vs-calcified distinction), V-Dem's **Neopatrimonial Rule Index directly captures extractive/calcified governance** through sub-indices for clientelism, presidentialism, and regime corruption. The clientelism index measures patron-client networks (access control calcification); exclusion indices track who governance systematically excludes by socio-economic group, gender, urban-rural location, political group, and social group. Conversely, the deliberative component, civil society participation, and equal access indices capture constructive governance. V-Dem also provides strong **gender-disaggregated variables** via the Women's Political Empowerment Index and its three sub-indices.

The recommended decomposition strategy for V-Dem is:

- **Z-density** = f(state territorial control, fiscal capacity, institutional layering, legislature existence, bureaucratic presence, party institutionalization)
- **Z-character (calcified)** = f(neopatrimonialism, clientelism, censorship, barriers to parties, CSO repression, exclusion indices, particularistic policy orientation)
- **Z-character (constructive)** = f(deliberation quality, consultation breadth, CSO participation, universalistic policy, equal access, judicial accountability)

**The Hanson & Sigman State Capacity Dataset** (Harvard Dataverse, 1960–2015, 177 countries) is the single most relevant existing dataset for z-axis density because it **explicitly measures extractive, coercive, and administrative capacity independently of regime type**. Its three dimensions—extractive capacity (tax revenue/GDP, fiscal capacity, census frequency), coercive capacity (military expenditure, territorial control), and administrative capacity (bureaucratic quality, statistical capacity)—map directly to governance reach and enforcement mechanisms. The deliberate separation of state capacity from democracy and development makes this uniquely suited to the framework.

**Polity5** (systemicpeace.org, 1800–2018) provides a simple −10 to +10 autocracy-democracy scale for 167 countries, but is **frozen at 2018** and far too coarse for this framework's needs. Its PARREG variable (regulation of participation) comes closest to measuring governance density in political domains. **Freedom House** (1972–present, 195 countries) and the **World Bank WGI** (1996–present, 200+ economies) both treat high scores as normatively positive and cannot directly serve the z-axis without inversion. The WGI's Regulatory Quality dimension is potentially invertible but conflates poor regulation with over-regulation.

The **OECD Product Market Regulation indicators** (1998–2023, 50+ OECD/partner countries) provide the **most direct measure of regulatory density/burden** in the economic domain, scoring restrictiveness on a 0–6 scale across state control, barriers to entrepreneurship, and barriers to trade. Higher PMR scores approximate "The Grip" in economic governance. The sector-level ETCR series extends back to 1975. Fraser Institute's Economic Freedom of the World (165 countries, 5-year intervals from 1970) and Heritage Foundation's Index of Economic Freedom (184 countries, from 1995) measure regulatory burden from a market-oriented perspective.

The **Quality of Government Dataset** (University of Gothenburg, qog-data, January 2026 version) compiles **~2,100 variables from 100+ sources** into a unified country-year format from 1946 to present—the most efficient single entry point for multi-source governance analysis.

## Where the calcification gap bites hardest

The most critical data gap is the absence of any standalone index measuring governance character—whether institutions serve broad interests or protect entrenched winners.

**Acemoglu & Robinson's inclusive-vs-extractive framework** has never been operationalized into a formal dataset. Their empirical work relies on existing indices (ICRG risk ratings, Polity scores, property rights measures) as proxies. The framework is binary rather than continuous and cannot easily distinguish absent governance from extractive governance. Over 50 empirical tests of **Mancur Olson's institutional sclerosis theory** have used indirect proxies—years of political stability, union density, number of interest groups—with "generally but certainly not universally" supportive results, but **no institutional sclerosis index has been created**.

The most promising operationalization is **"Measuring Open Access Orders"** (Murphy et al., published in *Journal of Institutional Economics*), which quantifies the North-Wallis-Weingast framework using the **harmonic mean of economic freedom (Fraser EFW), liberal democracy (V-Dem), and state capacity**. The harmonic mean ensures a country must score well on all three dimensions—an autocracy with high economic freedom does not qualify as an open-access order. This dataset covers **161 countries from 1950 to 2020**, with a supplementary series for 21 countries back to 1850. Only **35 of 161 countries** qualify as open-access orders in 2020. While this provides the closest existing proxy for the constructive-vs-calcified distinction, it remains a threshold classification rather than a continuous measure and cannot capture dynamic calcification within nominally open-access societies.

Other partial measures include: **The Economist's Crony Capitalism Index** (43 countries, irregularly from 2014), measuring billionaire wealth in rent-heavy sectors as a share of GDP—Russia leads at 19%; the **Ethnic Power Relations Dataset** (ETH Zurich, 800+ ethnic groups, 1946–2021), tracking political exclusion by ethnicity; **CIRI/CIRIGHTS Human Rights Data** (195 countries, 1981–2022), measuring government respect for 15+ human rights; and the **World Bank's State Capture Analysis methodology**, applied in Tunisia, Lebanon, and Indonesia but not yet as a cross-country index. The World Bank's Tunisia analysis found that Ben Ali regime-connected firms captured **~16% of total net private sector profits** through regulatory barriers—a direct quantification of extractive governance.

For constructing a composite z-axis, the optimal strategy combines V-Dem decomposition (1789+), Hanson & Sigman state capacity (1960–2015), the Open Access Orders measure (1950–2020), and OECD PMR for economic regulatory density (OECD countries, 1998+).

## Intersectional disaggregation is severely constrained before 1850

The framework's intersectional aspirations collide with data reality. **No single database enables race × gender × class × geography analysis across deep historical time.**

**IPUMS** (ipums.org) provides the strongest intersectional capacity: individual-level U.S. census microdata from **1850 to present** with detailed coding for race, sex, income, occupation, education, and sub-national geography—55+ million individuals across 13+ census years. IPUMS International extends partial microdata coverage to 100+ countries. For income/wealth distribution, the **World Inequality Database** (wid.world) provides percentile-level income and wealth shares for 100+ countries, some series from the 1800s, using Piketty's Distributional National Accounts methodology. The **SWIID** (199 countries, 1960–present) standardizes Gini coefficients across countries. V-Dem offers the strongest gender-disaggregated political data (1789+), and Barro-Lee provides gender-disaggregated education (1870+).

Pre-modern disaggregation is essentially limited to sex-based analysis of bioarchaeological data (the Global History of Health Project) and Seshat's coding of social stratification as a polity-level attribute. Seshat identifies the existence of social strata and discrimination but does not provide separate outcome data for each stratum. The honest assessment: **intersectional scoring before 1850 would require qualitative historical interpretation rather than quantitative database analysis**.

## Era-relative normalization requires a distance-to-frontier approach

No existing database scores capability relative to what was possible in each era. All measure absolute levels. The **Acemoglu-Aghion-Zilibotti distance-to-frontier framework** (2006, *Journal of the European Economic Association*) provides the strongest theoretical foundation for era-relative normalization. The method defines a frontier (typically the leading economy's TFP or GDP per capita) and scores each country as a ratio to that frontier. Özak (2018) extended this concept to pre-industrial periods by measuring travel time to technological frontiers across two millennia, finding persistent effects.

For the Vortical Progression y-axis, the recommended implementation is:

- **Define domain-specific frontiers for each era**: maximum observed life expectancy, GDP per capita, literacy rate, energy use per capita, and technological sophistication among all contemporaneous societies
- **Score each society as a fraction (0–100) of that era's frontier** in each domain
- **Weight and aggregate** domain scores into a composite y-score
- **Track the frontier trajectory separately** to capture technological acceleration

This sidesteps the HDI "goalpost problem" (UNDP uses fixed minima and maxima that compress scores as countries develop) and enables meaningful cross-era comparison: a society scoring 80/100 relative to its era's frontier in 1000 CE is genuinely comparable to one scoring 80/100 in 2000 CE, even though absolute capabilities differ by orders of magnitude.

For technological acceleration data supporting the exponential component, the strongest series are: OWID's transistor count compilation (1971–present, doubling every ~2 years); **Epoch AI's compute tracking** for AI training runs (doubling every ~3.4 months, far exceeding Moore's Law); Vaclav Smil's energy per capita estimates (deep history to present, showing a 5× increase from 1800 to 2000); the **USPTO Historical Patent Data Files** (1790–present, with 8.9 million patents through 2014); and the Bergeaud-Cette-Lecat TFP database (1890–present, 23 countries). Buringh & van Zanden's manuscript/printed book production data for Europe (6th–18th centuries) extends knowledge-production proxy measures into the pre-modern period.

## Conclusion: a feasible but gap-filled composite architecture

The full −10,000 BC to 2026 range demands splicing at least five temporal layers for each axis, with uncertainty growing dramatically before 1820. For the **y-axis**, the most practical architecture is: HYDE population + ACE complexity scores (−10,000 to −4,000 BC) → Seshat social complexity variables + GHHP bioarchaeological data (−4,000 BC to 1 CE) → Maddison GDP benchmarks + Allen wages (1 CE to 1500) → Clio-Infra multi-dimensional indicators (1500–1870) → AHDI composite (1870–2020). For the **z-axis**: ACE political integration scores (−10,000 to −4,000 BC) → Seshat administrative hierarchy + legal codes + centralization (−4,000 BC to 1789) → V-Dem decomposed indices + Open Access Orders measure (1789–present), supplemented by Hanson & Sigman state capacity (1960–2015) and OECD PMR (1998–present).

Three structural gaps cannot be filled with existing data. First, **pre-4000 BCE governance remains essentially unquantifiable** beyond coarse political integration scores from the Atlas of Cultural Evolution. Second, **no governance character index exists**—the constructive-vs-calcified distinction must be constructed from V-Dem component decomposition and Open Access Orders scoring rather than read off any existing dataset. Third, **intersectional disaggregation before 1850 is infeasible** at the quantitative level the framework envisions; pre-modern "intersectional" scoring would require qualitative historical reconstruction informed by bioarchaeological sex-disaggregated data and Seshat's stratification coding. The tight empirical correlation between capability and governance in Seshat data (75% shared variance) also challenges the two-axis model's independence assumption and deserves direct engagement—whether as an artifact of available data or a genuine empirical constraint on the framework's geometry.
